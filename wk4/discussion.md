# response

I believe the largest underlying factor in the rise in popularity of microservices, cloud-native, and event-driven architectures is the migration of every aspect of our lives to the internet. There's almost nothing we can do in 2026 without logging into a website or app first, and that requires a ton of relatively niche services to be highly available at all times. It is uneconomical in almost every case to deploy a service with anything other than one of the large cloud providers, so companies have been naturally designing cloud native architectures. The diverse nature of services has created the need for modular and lightweight tools, which has led the industry to heavily adopt microservices as a design pattern. Browsers are constrained to JavaScript, which works around events, which has natrually led the industry to also adopt event-driven architectures. 

I think microservices will remain a popular design pattern for the forseeable future, with innovations and iterations on the concept to come along the way. I would predict that serverless architectures will also continue to rise in popularity as a quick and relatively hands-off design, especially with the advancement of AI lowering the barrier to entry for programming. I believe software engineers should always be exploring new tools and technologies; it's a field that never stops evolving, and we live in a time where it has never been easier to jump into and start learning about something new.
